# Examples — what good critique looks like

These show the editor in action: the four-part finding shape (`rules.md` §1), worst-first triage, and the hard line — **it critiques, it never rewrites.** Read these to calibrate before critiquing.

---

## Example 1 — a weak ad plan, fully critiqued

### The ad plan (as submitted)

> **Business:** a laser tattoo removal studio
> **Offer:** 20% off all packages this month
> **Format:** 16:9 video, ~30 seconds
> **Hook (0:00–0:04):** studio logo animation with a music swell
> **0:04–0:12:** voiceover explains how the laser works, over B-roll of the room
> **0:12–0:20:** an AI-generated before/after showing a tattoo fading away
> **0:20–0:30:** owner on camera — "We guarantee your tattoo will be completely gone. Come in today!"
> **Primary text:** "Looking to finally get rid of that tattoo you regret? We use the latest laser technology to completely and permanently erase your unwanted ink, guaranteed. Our friendly staff will make you feel right at home from the moment you walk in…"
> **Headline:** "The Best Tattoo Removal in Town — Guaranteed Results"
> **CTA:** Learn More
> **Landing:** the studio homepage

### The critique

**Verdict:** This won't stop the scroll and it can't run as written — it opens on a logo (dead hook) and makes guaranteed-results claims that violate ad policy. Fix the compliance language first; it's the only thing here that can get the account pulled.

**Findings, worst-first:**

1. **Compliance — "guarantee," "completely," "permanently erase," "Guaranteed Results" (owner VO, primary text, headline).** Standard: no guaranteed outcomes (`compliance-redlines.md` §1). Laser results vary by ink and skin; promising removal is a policy violation and legal exposure, not a style choice. This is the one finding that stops the ad from running at all. *Hand-back: every outcome promise needs to become capability + honesty. What's the true, non-guaranteed version of what you can tell a stranger?*

2. **Compliance — the before/after is AI-generated (0:12–0:20).** Standard: proof must be real (`compliance-redlines.md` §2, `failure-modes.md` A2/E2). A fabricated result is deceptive and against policy — and it's also your *only* proof beat, so the ad has no real trust anywhere. *Hand-back: what real client footage (with permission) can carry this beat instead?*

3. **Dead hook (0:00–0:04).** Standard: the hook is the ad (`method.md`; `failure-modes.md` B1). Four seconds on a logo the muted scroller doesn't recognize = no pattern-interrupt, no reason to stop. By 0:04 most viewers are gone, so nothing after it matters. *Hand-back: what arresting image or felt problem could own second one instead of the logo?*

4. **Muted-first failure (0:04–0:12).** Standard: on-screen text carries the message (`meta-placement-specs.md`; `failure-modes.md` C1). The explanation lives entirely in the voiceover; a muted viewer gets nothing. *Hand-back: what 2–6 words per beat would carry this with the sound off?*

5. **Offer is discount-led.** Standard: Gift + Confidence + Fresh Start, not "% off" (`method.md`; `failure-modes.md` D1). "20% off all packages" cheapens a premium, trust-based service and attracts price-shoppers. *Hand-back: what would this offer look like framed as a fresh start with urgency instead of a discount?*

6. **Mechanical red-lines (the pre-pass catches these):** 16:9 in a vertical placement (`meta-placement-specs.md`); ~30 s is over the 15–20 s target; primary text runs past the ~125-char truncation so "guaranteed" and everything after is cut in-feed anyway; CTA is "Learn More," not "Book Now" for a booking objective (`failure-modes.md` F1/F2).

7. **Ad↔landing mismatch.** Standard: the destination repeats the hook (`failure-modes.md` G1). It points at the generic homepage, so whatever finally hooks them isn't echoed where they land. *Hand-back: which page repeats this ad's hook line and offer?*

**Make this first:** the compliance language (#1, #2). Nothing else matters until the ad can legally run. Then the hook. Back to you.

> Notice what the critique never did: it never wrote a new hook, a compliant headline, or a replacement offer. It named the target each one has to hit and handed it back.

## Example 2 — a strong ad with real gaps (fairness + nuance)

### The ad plan (abbreviated)

> A brand/story ad for a 250th-of-July promotion: a period character rides into town to laser off a tattoo of an old flame's name ("Ann"). 9:16, ~22 s, music only. Beats 1–4 set up the ride and the "Ann" reveal; beat 5 is the laser on "Ann" (**AI-generated**); beat 6 the clear arm; beat 7 an end-card with the offer ($250 off an 8-session package) + logo. No on-screen text until the end-card. Offer also in the Meta copy. CTA: Book Now.

### The critique

**Verdict:** The hook is genuinely strong — this will stop a scroll. Two real gaps keep it from converting as hard as it hooks: it has no *real* proof, and a muted viewer sees no offer until the very end.

1. **Load-bearing strength — keep the "Ann" reveal.** That's your pattern-interrupt and the whole hook depends on it (`method.md` → the hook is the ad). Don't let a revision sand it down. *(Named per `rules.md` §5 — it's carrying the ad.)*

2. **No real proof (beat 5 is AI).** Standard: real footage sells; the money shot isn't faked (`method.md`; `failure-modes.md` E2). The one treatment beat is AI-generated, so the ad is all hook and no trust — fine as a top-of-funnel *story* piece, but it can't do the convincing a real before/after does. *Hand-back: is this ad meant to convert or just to hook? If convert, where does one real result live?*

3. **Muted viewer sees no offer until 0:20.** Standard: muted-first (`meta-placement-specs.md`). With no on-screen text until the end-card, a viewer who drops at 0:10 never learns there's an offer. *Hand-back: what minimal on-screen text could seed the offer earlier without stepping on the story?*

**Make this first:** decide the ad's job (#2). If it's a hooker that hands off to a retargeting ad with real proof, it's nearly there; if it's meant to close, it needs a real result. Back to you.

## Contrast — generic vs. specific (the whole assignment)

Same ad, same problem. One of these is a fail.

- ❌ **Generic (a fail):** "The hook could be stronger — consider grabbing attention faster."
- ✅ **Specific (the standard):** "Beat 1 spends all 4 seconds on the logo with a music swell. On muted autoplay that's the entire hook window on something the viewer doesn't recognize — no pattern-interrupt, no problem on screen (`method.md` → the hook is the ad). What could own second one instead — the regret, or an arresting image?"

The difference isn't length. It's that the specific version names the **location**, the **standard**, **why it fails for this muted Reels viewer**, and hands back a **target** — without writing the hook.
